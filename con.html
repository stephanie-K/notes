Why I no longer believe in Content Design

“We can all write, why do we need a content designer?”

UX writing is the practice of crafting UI copy that guides users within a product and helps them interact with it. UI copy includes buttons and menu labels, error messages, security notes, terms and conditions, as well as any instructions on product usage.
The primary aim of UX writing is to settle communication between users and a digital product.

Work on text early because text problems often reveal design problems.
If a team has trouble explaining a design, quite often it is the design, not the text copy, that needs improving.

Tips On Writing UI Text

  • be concise
  • avoid long blocks of text
  • avoid double negatives
  • begin with the objective
  • use specific verbs whenever possible
  • make the copy consistent
  • avoid jargon
  • write in present tense
  • write in the active voice
  • Use numerals (number instead of their text version)
  • avoid showing all details up front (progressive disclosure is especially good for mobile UI)
  • identify interactive elements appropriately
  • be careful when using humor
  • use language that’s consistent with the user’s platform
  • use ‘today,’ ‘yesterday’ or ‘tomorrow’ instead of a date
  • use graphics if they will help you communicate

Goals in your research:

  • what do people want to do
  • what do they need help understanding
  • keep them in the know

8 common content design myths that content designers want you to stop believing

  • Content design is about words
  • Anyone can write content if they know about a subject
  • Once you have your design then you add your content
  • User needs and business needs are the same thing
  • Making content as detailed as possible saves everyone time in the end
  • Content design is the same as copywriting

Analytics and metrics

Scope - Using data to find out what content to improve

To stand out on a result page, you need 3 things:

  • push and pull content
  • trust
  • ease of use

you need to find:

  • the vocabulary your audience is using
  • what they want to know most (so you can prioritise
  • their mental models (how they thinks)

With metrics and analytics you can see:

  • how many people visit a page
  • how quickly people leave
  • most popular links on the page
  • time people spend on a page
  • search terms used on that page
  • where they came from (referrals)
  • where they go to

Be careful with bounce rate: it can mean someone landing on a page, not clicking on any of the links and leaving. Some count a bounce as someone arriving at a page and leaving in under 10 seconds. when looking at your data, bounce rate can help spot pages that aren't working effectively, but a high bounce rate cane also mean a page is working well!

Time on a page: this refers to how long a particular reader spends on a particular page. It might be seconds, or minutes. Like the bounce rate, you'll have to make an editorial judgment on whether time on a page is successful or not based on what the page is trying to do. If it's a page with a lot of text that you want people to read then higher time numbers are good. But if the page has a single call to action that most people should skip through, then high numbers are a warning sign that something is wrong with the page.

Headings are very important. I'ts the first thing your audience will see in the search results, so make them targeted and relevant. They are what will pull your audience to your site from search or, if already on the website, it will tell them they are in the right place.

Front-loading: putting the most important word(s) of the sentence at the beginning. If you frontload your headings, you make it easier and quicker for readers to understand the content.

Crit

Crit: short nickname for content critique

  • no set rules for how often you do crits
  • doing it early in the process stops people getting too attached to their copy before it's improved and helps everyone to be confident that they are heading in the right direction
  • you don't have to wait until after pair writing - you can do a crit at any stage of the process
  • take turn to be the note-taker
  • critique is not criticism. If content designers end up defending their work, the environment is wrong. Keep crits focused on making the product better
  • you don't have to take all the suggestions on board.Trust is needed that the content designer know what they are doing

Dates / Times

Asking for a date of birth

Time input #173 - Github

Maybe You Don’t Need a Date Picker

On errors: Slack

This particular message in the design system is a general message based on how something was validated in the service I worked on and we did not have a choice about how that happened. When we tested this people could always work out what had gone wrong and fix the problem.
Separate error messages, like 'Date of birth must include a day' and 'Date of birth must include a day and month', would be more helpful. It would make people validate a date in a specific way. We can add some more specific ones to the page. How is this?

#### If an incomplete date is entered and you know what is missing
Say ‘[whatever it is] must include a [whatever is missing]’.
For example, ‘Date of birth must include a month’ or ‘Date of birth must include a day and month’.

#### If an incomplete date is entered and you do not know what is missing
Say ‘Enter [whatever it is] and include a day, month and year’.
For example, ‘Enter your date of birth and include a day, month and year’.

Writing tips - From Sarah Richards

You probably have 3 seconds to get my attention, and 5 to keep it. so the first paragraph on a page is very very important. Make those 3 seconds count.

subheadings: they tell a story, it helps tell your audience in seconds exactly what information they are going to get. They break up the text and they help you remember and understand when the text is complex and long

Bullet points: they break things up, make the page less dense, make the text quicker to read

Keep sentences short about 15 to 20 words.

Jargon: introduce the word first. Writing to understand quickly has nothing to do with intelligent, it has a lot to do with time and respect.

The average reading age in the UK is about 9 years old. An average 9 years old has over 30,000 terms in their vocabulary. Writing for an age range isn't the same as writing to that age. Most 9 years old won't be interested in insurance. But someone who is 49 with little time or dyslexia or has a small phone screen or a life to live will benefit from you getting to the point quickly and with little jargon.
It's not dumbing down, it's opening up.

Respect your audience: if you write clearly with good structure, short sentences and plain language, you are helping people read. In the UK alone, this means you may be helping:

  • 1.5 million people who have a learning disability
  • 7 million people who have dyslexia
  • 2 million people with a visual impairment
  • lots of people using a poor internet connection
  • most people who don't want to read and reread something just to understand it

This is not about perceived intelligence - it's about speed of reading anf comprehension. Nothing else.
