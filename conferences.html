Martin Wright - Consequence scanning

A structured, repeatable process designed to extract, categorise and prioritise consequences. It’s a decision making process

we are so focus on delivering under tight deadlines that it cn be hard to think of the consequences - good or bad. Important to try to predict them so we can mitigate the bad ones and encourage the good ones

Consequences can be: unintentded, intended, good or bad. Not mutually exclusive: unintended is not always bad, intended ones not always good.

Work best if the idea is not too new, ideally work with a diverse group who understand what the idea is

the process:

  1. list the consequences
  2. classify consequences (good or bad, (un)intended)
  3. group consequences into categories (usually by theme or relation)
  4. prioritise consequences (high, medium, low)

After a consequence scanning exercise, you should be left with a prioritised, classified and categorised list of consequences agreed by the group.

Each consequence could require different approaches for example.

This process works best when participants can be honest and open

Consequence Scanning – an agile practice for responsible innovators

Bringing data to the service design table - collaborating to meet user needs and policy intents - Edafe Onerhime

Some questions you can start answering if you have the right data to measure the impact of your service:

  • Is your service equitable and easy to access for all citizens who need to use it?
  • Who is using your service right now? Are they the people you intended to help or do you need to rethink this?
  • where are the users of your public service located? Where d they live? Where do they work? Where do they access your public service?

Data is: text, numbers, images, anything we would like to store, it can be sound too. Anything we can analyse and use.

How do you bring data people to your service table?

  • Start where you are: you can reap the benefits of data no matter what state or stage your public service is in
  • Be clear on the why: you don't need all, or any of the answer. You do need to be clear on why this public service exists or is being commissioned.
  • Speak the same language: do you call a spade a spade? Is your customer actually a client? having a common language pays dividends for your team and for onboarding data people
  • Be an intelligent customer: have the 'domain expertise' on your team to explain clearly waht you're doing, why you're doing it, how things fit together, and where the gaps, risks, or known problems are
  • Make data literacy a team priority: everyone on your team should have basic data skills: or be put on the path to get them. Having a pool of people who know how to use data means less pressure on the 'data person'

Ley skills to look for in 'data people:

  • Communication: can they explain what data is needed for a general audience as well as a technical audience?
  • Analytical mindset: Can they help you break down what data you need, when you need it, and describe how it will help your public service be effective?
  • Knowledge of emerging technologies and solutions: do they have broad knowledge of what's avaialble to use or are they dedicated to using a single solution?'

Key take aways:

  • Public are under pressure-being informed by data will help your service swim not sink.
  • You don't need all the answers upfront - it helps to be an intelligent customer and build a data literate team.
  • This means starting where you are, being clear about why your service exists and speaking the same language

Long loop learning - Approaches for improvement without fast feedback - Elizabeth Ayer

A feedback loop is a system where an output is an input for future behaviour.

Feedback loops for services are all the things that happen between making a decision and using the result of that decision to make a new decision.

What if your serive only gets used once every year or longer? (Census for example)

Advices:

  • If you can shorten the time it takes to get through the loop, do it! (avoid being in a long loop!)
  • If you can't shorten the loop, but can still use plausible proxies, that's still good
  • But if you can't shorten the loop: it has to be easy to hand off person to person / easy to run indefinitely

Running a long expreriment: measuring impact

How to improve the inevitable survey - Caroline Jarret

The survey is a process for getting answers to questions from (a sample of) people for the purpose of getting numbers that you can use to make decisions

& steps as a linear process:

  1. Goal: establish your goals for the survey --> questions you need answers to
  2. Sample: decide who to ask and how many --> people you invite to answer
  3. Questions: test the questions --> questions people can answer
  4. Questionanaire: build the questionnaire --> questions people can interact with
  5. Filedwork: run the survey from invitation to follow --> people actually answer
  6. Responses: clean and analyse the data --> answers
  7. Reports: present the results --> decisions

Establish your goals for the survey:

  • What do you want to know
  • why do you want to know
  • What decision will you make based on these answers
  • What number do you need to make the decision

"I'm not sure" : where does this come from, is it an interaction issue? what is the person thinking?

Full Stack Service Design

We are asking users to be experts of our own system

We need to design our organisation and systems to work for users

Every organisation is a collection of micro design decisions, made on a daily basis that form our user experience.

Design thinking is something inherent within human cognition; it is a key part of what makes us human. (Nigel Cross – Design Historian)

We accept that things were the way they are because they’ve always been that way.

Conscious design: when we create design where we ask people to do thing in a certain way, we have considered it

Unconscious design, we don’t really think about the stuff, we don’t test it before we deliver it. This costs people. We have created systems that don’t solve problems. They create more work, cost us more to run and take us away from the frontline

You can have the best design ops, the best tools, designers and funding in the world, but the user experience can still be terrible.

We ALL have the ability to make conscious design decision that aid users. If our systems are to work, we have to learn how to consciously design together.

The fact that you have lots of designers do not necessarily means you have a user centred organisation.

On one side, there are the user experience, the business processes but on the other hand you also have the infrastructure,( the system and technology), the organisation and system (governance, finance, resources, procurement, organisational structure, skills and roles, culture and power, the intent, measurement and incentives, business model, policy, mission, values and ethics.

What good looks like?

Service are made of 1000s of decisions before we even get to the point of designing the ‘user experience’

Policy, government models, all these are material you can bend and play with when you design.

Bring the organisation with you.

User centred organisations are a weak link sport.

“to truly be design infused, the organisation must ensure every influencer of a project has that same design literacy and fluency” – Jared Spool

We must build a basic design literacy across organisations and systems to enable user experiences that work for people.

Organisations tend to sprint to build design and UX teams lifting and shifting services online, usually with a ‘digital lens’

Design team that moved from being dormant to be sprinting will be poorly commissioned by the organisation with hard delivery timescale, minimised budget, difficult policies to navigate.

But organisation that actually spend time in the starting block, will understand design, and has built buy-in across departments, designing from an end-to-end perspective.


Don’t build toolkit or over think ops, deliver value first, then learn how to repeat and scale what you did

First start building literacy and fluency in making design decisions at all levels of the stack by showing what good looks like and putting products and services in the hands of decision makers.

Defining a good project:

  • Feasibility (it is technically, operationally and financially feasible?
  • Measurable impact (can we achieve easy to measure impact in a short period?
  • Capability building (will this support the building of design capabilities and minds in the organisation?
  • Building a movement (will it build momentum and bring others on board across other departments?
  • Political and strategic persuasion (does it fit a strategic stream of work we can attach it to?
  • User experience (does it make an obvious improvement to the user experience?
  • Under the radar (do we need to work under the radar to give ourselves space to experiment?

User Vision - Deisgn for the future - 2019

World Usability Day 2019

Discussion of the panels – Insights

  • Roan Lavery (Free agent) You have to be aware of how your customers see you: Skyscanner see themselves as a travel agency, but the customers see them as ‘only’ flights sellers – something they are trying to change (Oli Mival – UR head at Skyskanner)
  • Blockbusters were seeing themselves as a DVD and video providers, and they failed to adapt, but Netflix position themselves as entertainment providers and could adapt much better
  • Oli Mival: UR is a Higher dimension being: “we reveal things that people can’t see themselves”
  • Approach the problem space with goals, what you want to find out, instead of hypotheses
  • Get an ideas bank/storage: you don’t know yet if it’s useful yet, but you might revisit this when the right technology becomes available (Oli Mival)
  • You need to present your idea in a mental model that people understand
  • Where to invest? What are the things that won’t change? This is where you should invest (your design principle is an example of things worth investing in as they won’t change)

Key Experience indicators

Require well thought out Goals, Signals and Metrics

  1. Goals - What is the outcome you are aiming towards?
    What are the product/service/feature goals that you want to ensure you are achieving?
    Use the KEI framework for inspiration if useful
  2. Signals - What indicates progress towards the goal?
    What shows that you are moving closer to (leading signals) or further from (lagging signals) your goal?
    Could be something the user is doing, thinking, or feeling
  3. Metrics - How do you effectively measure the signals?
    Should be something that is measurable that you can track over time, and you can take action as a result of

Golden rules:

  • KEI’s will never explain themselves. To understand a KEI, one must invest in qualitative research
  • Only measure things that you have the intent and ability to change
  • Measuring everything is measuring nothing. Focus on measuring that are truly central to the experience
  • Get agreement within the team that a certain KEI is important. Disagreement results in ignoring KEIs and not taking action. Create a shared understanding and acceptance of what’s being measured
  • Be careful not to measure behaviour through attitude. Don’t ask about behaviour in a survey. Instead, measure it directly
