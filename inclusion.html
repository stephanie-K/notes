Twitter thread: cognitive bias in content:

  • Cognitive biases are shortcuts the brain takes to make decisions. 95% of cognition is subconscious. Next time someone asks you why you're doing something, the most honest answer you can give is "how the hell should I know?"
  • Cognitive fluency is a thing. Clarity is king in product content strategy: Easy to read = easy to do, Hard to read = hard to do
  • Our own biases can be the biggest danger to users. Watch out for déformation professionnelle, the tendency to view the world through the eyes of our own profession. And the bias blind spot – we all have cognitive biases
  • The best way to recognise and overcome bias is to question our assumptions in a systematic way. And get really good at listening, because it's impossible to listen and react at the same time

Gendered intelligence - to increase understanding of gender diversity

Simon Croft, Director of Professional and Educational Services says: “At GI, we’re often asked for top tips for the workplace. Every organisation is different, but there are common themes that can help every setting increase its inclusivity.“

Mind Your Language!

Language is a central part of everyday interactions, and gender features frequently. Everything from names, pronouns and titles, to terms for identities, partners and relationships can all be gendered.

Using the right language demonstrates thoughtfulness and respect, but if we guess or assume, we’ll get it wrong some of the time. So establish a culture where people don’t assume what language to use about other people, but ask instead.

Set clear protocols such as ‘Start with gender neutral language’, ‘Ask, listen, respect’, and ‘If you make a mistake, apologise briefly and move on’.

We don’t guess people’s names – we ask and then do our best to remember. It’s simply an extension of that.

How to talk about gender at events

  • Sex and gender are different, don’t confuse the two: sex can be male / female / intersex, gender is woman, man and a lot in between
  • There are more than two genders
  • Do not assume people’s gender
  • If you’re running a ‘women’s’ event, think long and hard about why you’re excluding other underrepresented genders

thread on writing gender questions from a trans person who has designed surveys before

  • do you want to ask your research question about sex, or about gender, or about something else entirely? The best advice I can give is this: be specific
  • If you’re asking questions about uterine health, ask if your participant has a uterus, not for their gender
  • If you’re researching gender bias, ask about gender
  • If you simply need sex statistics, ask about sex
  • Think critically about what information you want
  • sex and gender are not the same
  • Sex describes a set of physical characteristics that are often associated with binary gender
  • Gender describes an individuals’ personal gender identity
  • Make sure you do not mix these up or conflate them!
  • One of the most common mistakes I see in this is listing “male” and “female” (and sometimes “intersex”) as genders. None of these are genders, all three of them are sex descriptors, and should only be used if you are asking a question about sex specifically
  • transgender is an adjective, not a noun or a verb. This means it can only be applied as an adjective, so a person can be transgender, but a person cannot be *a* transgender
  • it is transphobic to list “women”, “men”, “trans women” and “trans men”, when we say #TransWomenareWomen and #TransMenareMen we mean that creating a separate category for trans people is othering and exclusive. “Separate but equal” doesn’t work

Here’s the simplest possible options that I would recommend, when asking questions about sex and gender:

  • For sex, list options: male, female and intersex
  • For gender, list options: man, woman, nonbinary (write in)

And if you want to know if participants are trans? Ask that as a separate question, just as you would ask about any other adjective. You can simply ask “are you transgender”

More options for diversity are always best, but the above framework is the simplest possible framework.

In general, sex when applied to humans is NOT a particularly helpful categorization. Overall, it’s best practice to avoid asking about sex (instead ask specific anatomy or physiology questions) altogether if possible.
