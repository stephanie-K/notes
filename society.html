App for citizens, designed with citizens

As citizens experimented with the app’s features, they were able to stay in touch with io’s development team to report bugs and errors, provide feedback on the app’s operation and make constructive suggestions.

This resulted in an extremely valuable flow of information for us: people using the app are generally enthusiastic and satisfied with how it works, proving that the app really does meet their needs. The majority of bugs have surfaced, which ensures the release of a much more solid version of the io app, later this year.

The truly extraordinary thing, however, was that our roadmap evolved during these months of testing, thanks to citizen feedback. This is a radical paradigm shift: citizens are telling us what to work on, what features to develop, how to improve the app to better suit their needs.

Snook: Service design: creating a relational state

By making people partners in services, we can make those services more human. This is the right thing to do and, crucially, makes services more effective. Our challenge is to find ways to do this on a community-wide scale.

When we take this approach to a systematic level it discourages the individual and the community from counting on their own strengths and capabilities.

The ‘relational approach’ is based on the principle that humans are cooperative and collaborative and that if we can create the structures that facilitate this behaviour then we can create a better society.

Using neighbourhoods as the unit of change, experimenting with relational approaches, and focusing on strength-based service models.

While asset-based relational approaches may appear jargon-heavy, the principles are simple: services should be developed on the basis that ‘nothing for us or about us should be done without us’.

By stimulating what is already working, we encourage a system-wide approach to increasing physical activity in the borough that has designed itself to be unique to the individual needs of the diverse community it serves.

For Service Designers, this requires a radical shift in mindset from problems and needs to strengths and assets.

Facilitating communities of practice: from individual to ecosystem learning outcomes

Three key components of a community of practice:

  • the domain as “an identity defined by a shared domain of interest. Membership therefore implies a commitment to the domain, and therefore a shared competence that distinguishes members from other people”
  • the community as the pursuit of the community’s “interest in their domain” (op. cit.) and the relational space that leads to trust building — oftentimes through interactions that span the personal and the professional
  • the practice as the “shared repertoire of resources: experiences, stories, tools, ways of addressing recurring problems — in short a shared practice. This takes time and sustained interaction"

What does success look like when we design learning experiences that centre communities of practice?

  • Primary focus on relationships and trust building rather than on ‘knowledge transfer’
  • Coaches need coaches too. Keeping the flame alive takes time and energy and CoP stewards and facilitators also need support and nourishment to keep going
  • Design for ecosystem learning outcomes. There’s a need to think about the long-term journey of a community (emergent as it is!) and the role of a specific learning experience in the wider domain
  • Centre equity in the funding model. This is a big sticking point and experimental ground for me, as many learning offers come with a prohibitive cost. This usually reflects the amount of time, care and creative process that’s required to design and facilitate, while highlighting the lack of core funding to cover this.

How to talk about gender at events

  • Sex and gender are different, don’t confuse the two: sex can be male / female / intersex, gender is woman, man and a lot in between
  • There are more than two genders
  • Do not assume people’s gender
  • If you’re running a ‘women’s’ event, think long and hard about why you’re excluding other underrepresented genders
  • Do not ask people who are trans or nonbinary to educate you on how you should deal with gender.
